# Managing Australia’s GenAI Transition: A Policy Proposal for Clerical, Administrative and Entry-Level Knowledge Work
## Executive Summary
## Problem Definition and Context

Australia’s policy challenge is not sudden mass unemployment but a difficult-to-measure transition in how work is organised. Jobs and Skills Australia (JSA) argues that GenAI’s labour-market effects are still emerging and that Australia is early in a broader digital and AI transition, while the National AI Plan frames adoption as a process requiring deliberate, coordinated action if benefits are to be shared across regions, industries and communities (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; Department of Industry, Science and Resources 2025). Labour-market disruption may therefore appear first through changing tasks, hiring patterns and skill requirements rather than immediate job loss. The ILO finds that GenAI is more likely to augment occupations than fully automate them and that clerical work is the only broad occupational group with high exposure (Gmyrek, Berg & Bescond 2023). This makes clerical, administrative and entry-level knowledge roles a priority policy target because they contain routine information-processing tasks, serve as entry points into higher-skilled careers, and concentrate many workers with weaker adaptive capacity (Mandala 2024; Australian Digital Inclusion Alliance 2025).

## Analysis of Impacts

The most likely short-run effect of GenAI is task reorganisation rather than full job replacement. JSA reports that current GenAI systems have greater potential to augment work than automate it, although automation potential is higher in routine clerical and administrative tasks (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025). The ILO reaches a similar conclusion globally: clerical work is the only broad occupational group with high exposure, with around a quarter of clerical tasks highly exposed and a much larger share exposed at above-average levels (Gmyrek, Berg & Bescond 2023). This means tasks such as administrative drafting, scheduling, document handling and information retrieval may be partly automated, while workers shift toward checking outputs, handling exceptions and coordinating work.

Employment effects are likely to appear gradually through slower hiring and narrower entry pathways, rather than immediate occupational collapse. Evidence from online vacancies shows that AI-exposed firms reduce hiring in non-AI positions while changing the skills attached to remaining jobs, which is consistent with task substitution plus new skill demand (Acemoglu et al. 2021). Recent US evidence also points to disproportionate pressure on entry-level workers in highly exposed occupations (Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen 2025). For Australia, JSA is more cautious, arguing that large-scale displacement is not yet visible, but that entry-level roles could be disproportionately affected as adoption deepens (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

Wage effects are also likely to be uneven. In customer support, access to a GenAI assistant increased productivity by 14 per cent on average and by 34 per cent for novice and lower-skilled workers, with little improvement for highly experienced workers (Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond 2023). This suggests that GenAI can help less experienced workers perform tasks more effectively. However, productivity gains will not automatically become wage gains if firms capture most of the benefits or use GenAI to reduce demand for routine labour. Wage premiums may instead rise for workers who can supervise AI systems, validate outputs and manage complex exceptions.

Distributional and industry effects matter because exposure is not evenly spread. The ILO finds that GenAI’s effects are gendered because women are overrepresented in clerical roles, while Australian evidence highlights risks linked to digital exclusion, affordability and access (Gmyrek, Berg & Bescond 2023; Australian Digital Inclusion Alliance 2025). Exposure is also concentrated in information-intensive service sectors such as professional services, administrative support and finance, where GenAI may reshape internal job ladders even if total employment does not immediately fall (Mandala 2024). The key risk is therefore not only job loss, but a widening gap between workers and firms that can adapt to GenAI and those that cannot.

## Policy Options

Three policy options are available. They are assessed against four criteria: efficiency, equity, feasibility and speed.

**Option 1: Targeted transition support.** Government funds short-form training, AI literacy, digital foundation skills, career guidance and job-matching support for clerical, administrative and entry-level workers through existing TAFE, VET and higher-education channels. This option scores strongly on equity and speed because it directly supports workers most exposed to task change. However, its efficiency depends on whether courses are closely linked to real vacancies and employer demand. If training is too general, it may improve awareness without improving job mobility (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

**Option 2: Employer co-investment.** Government uses matched grants, tax incentives or procurement conditions to encourage firms to retrain workers, redesign jobs responsibly and consult staff during AI implementation. This option scores strongly on efficiency because employers understand their own task structures and technology needs. OECD evidence also suggests that training and worker consultation are important for better workplace outcomes during AI adoption (OECD n.d.). However, this option is weaker on equity because firms may focus training on existing high-performing workers and underinvest in junior or lower-skilled employees.

**Option 3: National stewardship and inclusion.** Government establishes a national mechanism to monitor GenAI adoption, skill demand, entry-level hiring and transition risks, while also addressing digital exclusion among women, regional communities and disadvantaged workers. This option is strongest on long-run adaptability and equity because it improves labour-market intelligence and helps government identify where support is needed. Its weakness is speed: monitoring and coordination are necessary, but they do not immediately retrain workers or change employer behaviour (Australian Digital Inclusion Alliance 2025; Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

| Option | Efficiency | Equity | Feasibility | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Targeted transition support | Medium | High | Medium | High |
| Employer co-investment | High | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| National stewardship and inclusion | Medium | High | High | Low |

A mixed package is preferable because each option addresses a different weakness. Targeted transition support helps workers adjust quickly, employer co-investment links training to actual workplace change, and national stewardship ensures the response can adapt as evidence improves. Relying only on training would risk weak employer uptake, while relying only on firms would risk unequal access. Combining the three options gives government a more balanced response to efficiency, equity and feasibility.
