# Managing Australia’s GenAI Transition: A Policy Proposal for Clerical, Administrative and Entry-Level Knowledge Work
## Executive Summary

Generative AI is reorganising Australian work rather than eliminating whole occupations. The policy problem is uneven adjustment in clerical, administrative and entry-level knowledge work, where routine drafting, document processing and information retrieval are highly exposed. Without intervention, firms may capture productivity gains while reducing junior hiring opportunities and widening digital inequality. This proposal recommends targeted transition support, employer co-investment in retraining and job redesign, and national stewardship to monitor adoption and entry-level hiring. This package balances productivity, equity and adaptability while avoiding overreaction to still-emerging labour-market effects.

## Problem Definition and Context

Australia’s policy challenge is not sudden mass unemployment, but uneven adjustment as GenAI changes work organisation, hiring and skill requirements. Jobs and Skills Australia (2025) argues that GenAI is more likely to augment jobs than replace them, although impacts will vary across occupations, industries, regions and groups. The National AI Plan similarly frames AI as a productivity opportunity, but emphasises coordinated action so gains are widely shared (Department of Industry, Science and Resources 2025). Early action can reduce adjustment costs before displacement appears in unemployment statistics.

The policy failure is that private adoption incentives are not aligned with social adjustment costs. Firms may reduce routine labour costs but underinvest in training, junior pathways and consultation because these benefits spill over to the wider labour market. OECD evidence suggests GenAI can raise demand for higher skills and that workers and smaller firms need support to adapt (OECD 2025). Clerical, administrative and entry-level knowledge workers should therefore be prioritised because their jobs include routine information-processing tasks such as drafting, classification, scheduling and customer support. If firms reduce junior hiring, younger workers and workers with lower digital capability may find it harder to build experience.

## Analysis of Impacts

The most likely short-run effect is task reorganisation rather than full job replacement. JSA reports that current GenAI systems have greater potential to augment work than automate it, although automation potential is higher in routine clerical and administrative tasks (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025). The ILO similarly identifies clerical work as the broad occupational group with the highest exposure (Gmyrek et al. 2023). AI can draft, retrieve and classify information, while humans remain responsible for judgement, verification and exceptions.

Employment effects are likely to appear through slower hiring and narrower entry pathways rather than occupational collapse. Vacancy evidence shows that AI-exposed firms change skill demand and reduce hiring in some non-AI roles (Acemoglu et al. 2022). Recent US evidence suggests early-career workers in highly AI-exposed occupations may face weaker employment outcomes (Brynjolfsson et al. 2025). For Australia, large-scale displacement is not yet visible, but entry-level roles could still be affected (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

Wage effects are also likely to be uneven. In customer support, a GenAI assistant increased productivity by about 14 per cent on average and by 34 per cent for novice and lower-skilled workers (Brynjolfsson et al. 2023). However, gains may not become wage gains if firms capture benefits or reduce routine labour demand. Wage premiums may rise for workers who supervise AI, validate outputs and handle exceptions.

Distributional effects matter because exposure is uneven. The ILO finds gendered effects because women are overrepresented in clerical roles, while Australian evidence highlights digital exclusion and affordability risks (Gmyrek et al. 2023; Australian Digital Inclusion Index 2025). Mandala Partners (2024) estimates that GenAI could affect the skills composition of 7.2 million Australian workers. The key risk is a widening gap between those that can adapt and those that cannot.

## Policy Options

Three policy options are assessed against efficiency, equity, feasibility and speed.

**Option 1: Targeted transition support.** Government funds short-form training, AI literacy, career guidance and job-matching through TAFE, VET and higher education. This is strong on equity and speed, but efficiency depends on courses being linked to vacancies and employer demand (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

**Option 2: Employer co-investment.** Government uses matched grants, tax incentives or procurement conditions to encourage firms to retrain workers, redesign jobs and consult staff. This is efficient because employers understand their task structures, but less equitable if firms prioritise high-performing workers. OECD evidence supports training and consultation during AI adoption (OECD 2024).

**Option 3: National stewardship and inclusion.** Government monitors GenAI adoption, skill demand, entry-level hiring and digital exclusion. This supports adaptability and equity, but does not immediately retrain workers or change employer behaviour (Australian Digital Inclusion Index 2025; Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

| Option | Efficiency | Equity | Feasibility | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Targeted transition support | Medium | High | Medium | High |
| Employer co-investment | High | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| National stewardship and inclusion | Medium | High | High | Low |

A mixed package is preferable because each option addresses a different weakness. Transition support helps workers adjust, employer co-investment links training to workplace change, and stewardship helps policy adapt as evidence improves. Combining all three balances efficiency, equity and feasibility.

## Recommended Package and Implementation Plan

A sequenced mixed package should be adopted. DEWR should lead, JSA should monitor exposed occupations, the Department of Industry, Science and Resources should align the package with the National AI Plan, and TAFE, VET and higher-education providers should deliver training. Larger firms and public agencies should co-fund retraining and consult workers when GenAI changes task design or entry-level recruitment (Department of Industry, Science and Resources 2025; Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; OECD 2024).

**Phase 1 (0–12 months):** establish stewardship, identify priority occupations, launch pilot short courses and publish a transition dashboard. Pilot funding should be capped and targeted to exposed workers, with higher subsidy rates for SMEs, regional providers and disadvantaged workers. Training should cover AI literacy, output verification, privacy and job-search support.

**Phase 2 (1–3 years):** scale successful courses, embed AI capability into qualifications and expand employer partnerships. Larger firms should move toward matched co-funding. Supported employers should report training participation, consult workers and preserve or redesign entry-level pathways.

**Phase 3 (3+ years):** review course quality, employment outcomes and entry-level hiring. If risks persist, government should consider stronger reporting, procurement rules or data-policy settings.

Success should be measured through training completion, employer participation, job-to-job mobility, re-employment outcomes, entry-level hiring and digital inclusion gaps.

## Risks & Mitigations

The main risk is weak employer uptake. Firms may adopt GenAI without enough retraining or job redesign. This can be mitigated through matched funding, procurement incentives and guidance (OECD 2024; Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

A second risk is widening inequality. Workers with lower digital access or weaker foundation skills may benefit less from GenAI. This requires targeted subsidies, place-based delivery and digital inclusion measures (Australian Digital Inclusion Index 2025).

A third risk is AI quality and safety. Without safeguards, GenAI may create errors, bias, privacy breaches and weak records. Training should include output verification, privacy, bias awareness and accountability (OECD 2024).

A fourth risk is policy lag. GenAI may change faster than training systems can respond. Annual JSA reporting, shorter curriculum cycles and pilot reviews would let government adjust as evidence improves (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).
